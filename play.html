AI Product GTM & Internal Adoption Campaign 2024

Kuaishou · Strategy, Community, Event, Data

Context

Kuaishou is one of China's leading short-video platforms. In June 2024, the company launched an internal AI productivity suite aimed at accelerating company-wide AI transformation — moving AI capabilities from R&D into everyday business operations. I joined as an AI Product Marketing Intern within the Efficiency Engineering team, working alongside product, R&D, and community leads.

Challenge

For non-technical employees, the barrier was conceptual: no vocabulary, no use-case guidance, no confidence to integrate AI into existing workflows. For developers, API call volume remained low and the agent ecosystem was underdeveloped. The opportunity was to reach both cohorts simultaneously through a single campaign architecture.

Architecture — 4-Phase GTM

Campaign structure

Phase 01
Brand Awareness
Internal launch communications. AI positioned as enabler, not threat.
Phase 02
Feature Intro & Rollout
SQL + R segmentation to identify high-potential cohorts. Targeted onboarding flows.
Phase 03
AI Hackathon
2-month campaign. Custom platform, space-themed narrative, offline finale.
Phase 04
KOL Workshops & Community
6 live workshops. Self-reinforcing community. Cold-start playbook codified.

What I Did

GTM strategy — Designed the 4-phase adoption framework; used SQL and R to segment user behaviour and prioritise high-potential cohorts for targeting, informing each phase's rollout decisions.

AI Hackathon — Conceived and led a 2-month campaign built around an internal AI Hackathon: space-themed narrative, custom online interactive platform for real-time agent submission and peer reaction, and an offline finale coordinated with PM and R&D.

KOL & community — Identified and recruited core power users through targeted outreach, then ran 6 live workshops to build skills and sustain engagement — creating a self-reinforcing community that continued beyond the campaign. Documented findings into a reusable cold-start playbook.

Results

4× Peak DAU increase
1,000+ Participant interactions
3× Community engagement
£1.2m Downstream project revenue

What I Learned

Gamification works not just for engagement but as a diagnostic tool — the Hackathon surfaced which use cases resonated most, directly informing Phase 3 targeting decisions. SQL segmentation told me who to target; the KOL workshop sessions told me why they were hesitant. The combination proved more powerful than either alone.

B2B Content Strategy & Brand Building from Zero 2024

Coohom · Research, Strategy, Writing

Context

Coohom Cloud is a unicorn-stage spatial intelligence company specialising in synthetic 3D data for robotics and AI training. I joined to build Coohom's international B2B presence from zero — targeting AI researchers and robotics engineers in American and European markets. The LinkedIn account had fewer than 200 followers and no established content strategy.

Challenge

Synthetic data for robotics is technically complex, and the target audience — researchers, engineers — is notoriously resistant to traditional marketing. The challenge was to build credibility fast enough to matter at CVPR and ICRA 2024, while establishing a content engine that would outlast my internship.

Architecture — 6-Category Content Framework

Content categories mapped to funnel stage

Awareness

What I Did

Competitor audit — Analysed Scale AI, Rendered.ai, CVEDIA, and Parallel Domain on LinkedIn. Top performers led with use-case storytelling rather than product specs — this shaped Coohom's differentiation angle.

Content architecture — Built the 6-category framework (Solution, Tool, Class, Event, Info, Day) mapped to funnel stages. Translated into a full posting calendar for March–August 2024.

Optimisation loop — Monitored backend analytics weekly. Identified that use-case posts consistently outperformed product announcements; restructured the schedule accordingly. High-performing format: research bottleneck → Coohom as the solution.

Results

1,000+ LinkedIn followers (from <200)
300× Impressions growth in 4 months
20 Qualified leads at CVPR & ICRA
400 Branded giveaways distributed

What I Learned

Systematising early is what made the 300× growth possible in 4 months rather than 12. The 6-category framework was designed to be self-sustaining: each category type served a different funnel function, so the calendar could run even with limited resources. Technical audiences don't respond to marketing language — they respond to peers and problems.

Integrated Event Marketing — CVPR & ICRA 2024 2024

Coohom · Event, Lead Generation, Content

Context

As part of Coohom's international expansion, I was tasked with representing the brand at CVPR (Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition) and ICRA (International Conference on Robotics and Automation) in May–June 2024. Both conferences attract thousands of researchers, engineers, and decision-makers globally. No existing playbook, no prior lead-gen process, near-zero name recognition.

Challenge

Conference booths are passive by default. The challenge was converting transient foot traffic into meaningful engagement and measurable leads without a large budget or established brand name — and making offline impact traceable online.

Architecture — 3-Phase Campaign Arc

End-to-end campaign structure

Phase 01
Pre-event
  • 4-week content series on LinkedIn
  • Industry trend hooks + exhibition announcements
  • Game teaser + solution showcases
  • Audience anticipation building
Phase 02
On-site
  • Gamified lead-gen mechanic (Catch Master)
  • QR code → LinkedIn on booth desktops
  • Branded giveaway distribution (400 units)
  • Structured lead capture
Phase 03
Post-event
  • ICRA 2024 press release authored + published
  • SEO backlink asset on company site
  • Analytics review → content strategy update
  • Lead handoff to sales team

What I Did

Gamified lead-gen — Independently proposed and designed a game-controller-shaped flyer inviting attendees to play "Catch Master" — a custom booth game where high scorers received branded giveaways. Gave the team a natural conversation starter; turned passive visitors into active participants.

Online-offline loop — Placed QR codes on booth desktops linking to Coohom's LinkedIn, encouraging visitors to post with campaign hashtags — creating a traceable bridge between physical presence and measurable online growth.

Content & logistics — Authored the ICRA 2024 post-event press release published on the company website. Managed end-to-end sourcing of 400 branded giveaways (LEGO sets, USBs), including vendor comparison, procurement, and on-time delivery to two international venues.

Results

400 Branded giveaways distributed
20 Qualified leads generated
2 International conferences covered

QR-to-LinkedIn integration contributed directly to follower growth during the conference period. Post-event press release published as an ongoing SEO-linked content asset.

What I Learned

The game wasn't just entertainment — it was a filtering mechanism. People who chose to play were already curious enough to engage, making subsequent conversations more qualified. Designing the lead-gen mechanic around the audience's identity (engineers who enjoy solving problems) was the key insight. Making the digital bridge explicit turned a time-limited event into an ongoing audience-building moment.

Discourse Analysis of "Vibe Coding" on Platform X In progress · 2026

Cambridge MPhil · Digital Humanities, Discourse Analysis, Social Network Analysis

Context

"Vibe Coding" — coined by Andrej Karpathy (OpenAI co-founder) and named Collins Dictionary's Word of the Year 2025 — describes a new paradigm in AI-assisted software development where users express high-level intent in natural language rather than writing code directly. This dissertation examines how the term diffused and transformed across Platform X, drawing on Actor-Network Theory (Latour) and Science and Technology Studies.

Research Questions

What are the prevailing topics surrounding "vibe coding," and what are the identity characteristics of the groups driving each discourse? Which rhetorical strategies — modalities, citation of data, appeal to authority — do different stakeholder groups use to legitimise their position? How does discourse evolve through networked interactions?
